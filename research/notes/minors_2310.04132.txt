                                           The elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem is of fundamental importance in public-key cryptography.
                                       It is in use for a long time. Moreover, it is an interesting challenge in computational mathematics. Its
                                       solution is supposed to provide interesting research directions.

                                           In this paper, we explore ways to solve the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem. Our results are
                                       mostly computational. However, it seems, the methods that we develop and directions that we pursue
                                       can provide a potent attack on this problem. This work follows our earlier work, where we tried to solve
                                       this problem by finding a zero minor in a matrix over the same finite field on which the elliptic curve is
                                       defined. This paper is self-contained.

2 Generate A

The central idea behind constructing A is to look at a matrix M and construct its left-kernel K. Recall
that the left-kernel is the kernel of the transpose of a matrix. Then A is just a sub-matrix of K. The idea
behind constructing M is a well known theorem. Before we go to the theorem, we need to be familiar with
the concept of a divisor on E.

Definition 2 (Divisor). Let E be the elliptic curve defined over F. The divisor D is defined as the formal
sum D = i ni[Pi], where ni are integers and Pi are rational points on E. Since the field F is finite, the
formal sum is always finite.

    We present bare minimum details on divisor in this paper, enough to make it self contained. For more
on divisors the reader can consult any text book in algebraic geometry. Fulton [2], Hartshorne [3], Milne [5]
and Silverman [6] all contain such details.

    If ni  0 the divisor is called effective. The group of all divisors D is a free abelian group and is called
the divisor class group. When the divisors belong to the elliptic curve E it is denoted by Div(E). The degree
of the divisor D is i ni. The set of all degree zero divisors form a subgroup of the group of divisors and is
denoted by Div0(E).

    Let f be a homogeneous rational function in P2. Then f = g/h where g and h are homogeneous
polynomials in three variables of the same degree and furthermore E does not divide h. Then f defines a
rational function on E. The points of intersection of g are zeros of f (counting multiplicity) and the points of
intersection of h and E are poles of f (counting multiplicity). Over a algebraically closed field, the number of
zeros and poles of f are finite and are the same. Then one can define the divisor corresponding to f as Df =

   ni[Pi] where g intersects E at Pi with intersection multiplicity ni or h intersects E at Pi with multiplicity
ni. Note, the multiplicity of a zero is considered positive and that of a pole negative. These divisors are
called principal divisors and denoted by Prin(E). We define the Picard group Pic0(E) = Div0(E)/Prin(E).
One can now define a map from the group of rational points of an elliptic curve E to the group Pic0(E).
This map is know as the Abel-Jacobi map.

                  P  [P] - [O]                                                                                        (2)

For a non-singular elliptic curve over an algebraically closed field this map is an isomorphism.
    Let C be a projective plane curve of degree n not containing E. Then according to Bezout's theorem

the curve C can intersect E in at most 3n points (counting multiplicity). We use d = 3n for the rest of the
paper.

    We now state and sketch a proof of a theorem that was proved in Mahalanobis et al. [4] (see also [1]).
We include a short proof for the convenience of the reader.

Theorem 2.1. Let E be as defined above. Then there is a plane projective curve C passing through d points,

P1, P2, . . . , Pd of E and not containing E if and only if  d    Pi  =  O.  Where  O  is  the  neutral  element  of  the

abelian group E.

Proof. The proof over algebraically closed field is straightforward consequence of the fact that the Abel-

Jacobi map is an isomorphism. We pay special attention to the part where there is a plane projective curve
C of degree n that intersects the elliptic curve E at d points. We construct the rational function C/zn.

Now this rational function has a pole of order d at O. Thus the Abel-Jacobi map says that i Pi = O. The
reverse direction is straightforward.

    Extending the proof to finite fields uses a well known trick using the Frobenius automorphism of a finite

field.

    Coming back to the discrete logarithm problem, we need to compute m from P and Q. We intend to

compute unique points of the form niP = Pi and -njQ = Qj where ni and nj are integers in the range 1 to
p, such that, i Pi + j Qj = O. This gives us i ni - m j nj = 0 mod p. We know ni and nj and then
we have a linear equation in one unknown m. Thus we can solve for m and the discrete logarithm problem

is solved.

by exhaustive search.

    The next theorem is of vital interest for the algorithm that we develop in this paper. In this case we
take l extra points. In other words, the matrix M created above now has d + l rows constructed out of
d + l points Pi and Qj. From this matrix, the left-kernel K can be computed easily. The columns of this
left-kernel K corresponds to the points Pi and Qj on E from which M was created. For the experiments we
take n = log2 p. In practice one can take n to be some constant multiple of log2 p where the constant is a
small integer. When this is the case, computing K from M is polynomial in both space and time complexity.

Remark 1. In the above theorem we are talking about l zeros. Why the number of zeros can not be different
than l?

    In our experience with computing the discrete logarithm problem, we have never seen this happen except

in one case. This is when a chosen point ni is the secret m. This is tantamount to solving the discrete
logarithm problem by guessing and never happens for large enough prime p. The other reason is theoretical.
If there is a vector v with more than l zeros then that vector is non-zero in less than 3n places. We create
a sub-matrix M of M corresponding to the non-zero position in v. The number of these positions is less

than 3n and thus the number of rows in M is less than 3n. We extend this matrix M to 3n rows by
putting in arbitrary rows corresponding to arbitrary points from E. Then the non-zero components of v
along with few zeros at appropriate places will be in the left-kernel of this extended matrix. Then the sum
of the points that make the rows of this extended matrix will be O. This is absurd as the rows that extend
M was arbitrarily chosen. Thus the number of zeros never exceed l. However, if there are fewer than l
zeros then the curve C so obtained contain the elliptic curve E and is thus useless for our purpose. From the
matrix K, the matrix A is extracted.

Remark 2. How do we ensure that there is atleast one Pi and one Qj in the non-zero entries of v created
above?

The above is an important point because unless both Pi and Qj are present in the non-zero entries of

the v produced above, the discrete logarithm problem is not solved. However, there are many ways to solve

this problem. First, in our experiments we optimize d and l. It is well known that  d+l  is the biggest when

3 A zero-minor solves the discrete logarithm problem

In this section, we show that finding a zero-minor in A solves the discrete logarithm problem by showing
that there is one-one correspondence between zero-minors in A and maximal zero-minors in K. Note that
K is a l × 2l matrix and A is a l × l matrix. Recall that for the purpose of experiments we are assuming
that d = l.

    The size of K and A follows from the fact that the left-kernel K is a l dimensional vector space. The basis
matrix of the subspace K is denoted by K as well; where rows and columns of K are indexed by {1, 2, . . . , l}
and {1, 2, . . . , 2l} respectively.

    Each row of the matrix K is a basis vector of the subspace K. Then one can do row operations to reduce
K into the following form:

                                     a11 a12 a13 . . . a1l 0 . . . 0 1

                                     a21 a22 a23 . . . a2l 0 . . . 1 0                               (3)
K=
                                     ...  ...  ...  ...  ...  0    ...  ...  ...  
                                                                                  
                                                                                  

                                     al1 al2 al3 . . . all 1 . . . 0 0

where the right hand side is in the reverse identity form, which we will refer to as the sparse part. The
left hand side is the dense part which is A. If any one of aij = 0 where i, j = 1, 2, . . . , l then the discrete
logarithm problem is solved. So we can safely assume that aij = 0 for all i, j.

    By a maximal minor in K we mean a minor of size l, in which case all the rows are chosen and l columns
from 2l columns are chosen. Let {a1, a2, . . . , ak} be the rows and {b1, b2, . . . , bk} be the columns of a zero-
minor in A. Let {a1 , a2, . . . , al-k} be the ordered complement of the set {a1, a2, . . . , ak} in {1, 2, . . . , l}.
Then the maximal minor in K consist of the columns {b1, b2, . . . , bk, l + a1 , l + a2, . . . , l + al-k}. To see why
this is true, notice that if there is a zero-minor in A, then there are row operations in A that gives a zero
row in the sub-matrix of the minor. Use the same row operations on K. Now note in the sparse part of K
the position of the one is (i, l + i) for each i  {1, 2, . . . , l}. The row operation changes the structure of the
sparse part of K. The zero-row in the sub-matrix gives rise to a non-zero part of size k in the sparse part.
So the maximal minor is the columns in A and then the part in the sparse part of K that does not contain
the non-zero part we just talked about, ordered accordingly. An observant reader might argue that what
happens if all the rows of the sub-matrix of the minor were not used to get the zero-row. In that case we
will have more than l zeros in a row and then a look at Remark 1 says that it can not be the case. So all
rows must be involved. Thus we have proved the following theorem.

                                                    4
Theorem 3.1. For every zero-minor arising from a proper sub-matrix of A there is a maximal zero-minor
in K. Furthermore, if there is a maximal zero-minor in K then that solves the discrete logarithm problem.

Proof. The first part is already proved. To prove the last part of the theorem, first note that the maximal
minor of K is of size l. Then the maximal zero-minor of K gives rise to a vector in the left-kernel with
exactly l zeros; Theorem 2.2 takes care of the rest.

4 Finding a zero-minor in A

In this section, we talk about methods and algorithms to find a zero minor in the l × l matrix A which is
non-singular. It is easy to see that if A is singular the discrete logarithm problem is solved. The number
of minors in A is exponential in the size of the matrix. For a square matrix of size l, just the number of
principal minors is 2l - 1. The number of total minors far exceed that. Thus, it is not possible to test all
minors.

    The purpose of our experiments in this paper is to solve this obvious conundrum ­ how to talk about all
minors without involving all of them? The modus operandi for our experiments is the following:

 Fix a set of minors. Then look for a zero minor in this set.

 Fix an elliptic curve over a finite field of characteristic 2 and two points P and Q.

 Create a left-kernel K. This part is randomized. Then create the submatrix A from K.

 Check for zero minor in the fixed set of minors in A. If one found, the program stops.

 Repeat with a new kernel K until a zero minor is found in the fixed set of minors and count the number
       of kernels needed.

We used three fixed sets. One, the set of all 2-minors. Two, the set that comes out of Gaussian elimination
and Schur complement and the third is the set of almost principal minors. The size of all these sets are
polynomial in the size of the matrix A. We report our results and subsequent improvements.

    We started by testing all 2×2 minors of A for small enough fields. We call this experiment the all 2 minor
experiment and was reported in our earlier work [1]. We provide the result for this experiment in Table 1.

4.1 Initial Minors

The purpose of this paper, is to show experimentally that probably there is a small set of minors and it is
enough to check that set for a zero minor. We call this the initial minor conjecture and is from our earlier
work [1]. This is the reason that we are experimenting with a fixed set of minors. The idea is to somehow
approximate this set. Gain some understanding about it before we can formally prove its existence.

    Conjecture (Initial Minors). For a matrix A, there exists a set of minors, whose cardinality is
bounded by a polynomial or a sub-exponential function in the size of the matrix. If all the minors in this set
is non-zero then all minors of the matrix is non-zero as well. This set is called a set of initial minors.

not a significant subset. Motivated by the Gaussian elimination Schur complement algorithm, in the next

5 Almost principal minor algorithm

In the Gaussian elimination Schur complement experiment, a 2 × 2 zero minor from H was augmented with
the principal block matrix E. This augmented minor was then used to solve the discrete logarithm problem.
If the augmented zero minor had k rows and columns, the block matrix E has dimensions (k - 2) × (k - 2).
The block matrix E has contiguous row and column indices. Moreover these row and column indices are

identical. Such a minor is referred to as a principal minor.

5.1 Experiments with almost principal minor

This experiment used the fixed set of almost principal minor to search for a zero minor. The input to
this algorithm consists of a matrix A as described earlier. These matrices are over binary fields of sizes
from 25 to 47 bits. For each input we count the number of kernels required before the first zero minor is
found. Then the experiment is repeated ten times. The size of E is a prime p and about the same size of
the field. The average number of kernels required to solve the discrete logarithm problem is then recorded
for each binary field. Averaging over ten attempts is used to minimize the effect of randomization, as a
randomized algorithm can sometimes solve the problem quickly or take a longer time for the same input.
If the number of kernels grows gradually as inputs size increase, then ten attempts should be sufficient to
minimize randomization effects. This was the case in our experiments, thus averaging over ten attempts is
effective.

6 Conclusions

In this paper, we continued developing a novel method to solve the elliptic curve discrete logarithm prob-
lem [1, 4]. Our method is unique as it uses minors to solve the discrete logarithm problem. Furthermore, we
tried to establish the initial minor conjecture on a firm footing. We find this conjecture interesting.

    At the heart of our method is partition of m modulo p into d parts. The process is simply to find integers
ni for i = 1, 2, . . . , d where each 0 < ni < p such that i ni = 0 mod p. However, not any partition will
do. Since we want to solve the discrete logarithm problem, both P and Q should be involved. Which means
that m should divide some of these ni, but not all. Since, we do not know m, this is a hard problem. The
way we handled this problem is by going to curves C intersecting the elliptic curves E at d points. The extra
points theorem gave us the leverage to move into zero minors.

    Now once we see this process as a whole, then one partition of m will give rise to several partitions of m.
One such possibility is that for a non-zero c, such that, 0 < c < p. Now consider ni = ni - c and nj = nj + c
for two different i and j, which are not divisible by m. This will give rise to a different set of points on E
and subsequently another partition of m. Of course, there will be other partitions related to the original
partition that have no explicit and easy description like the one above.

    But, our algorithm is probabilistic in nature. We have no control on the points ni that we choose. They
are chosen at random. On the other hand, there are simply so many related partitions that some of them
are bound to occur. In other words, once we see one zero minor, there will be many zero minors related to
the original zero minor. This was also observed experimentally.

    In the following experiment we took the finite field of size 225. We computed a left-kernel K and then A
for an appropriate choice of elliptic curve and points Pi and Qj as described earlier. The goal was to find
all zero minors in A. It turned out to be impossible. So we used a short cut. We took contiguous principal
